Theory of Operation

This page explains the RF theory behind the HF Vertical Designer. It covers impedance modeling, transformer behavior, coax transformation, SWR math, ERP estimation, and how height and ground conductivity shape radiation patterns.

1. Wavelength & Electrical Length

The foundation of all antenna modeling is wavelength:

λ (meters) = 300 / frequency(MHz)

1.1 Electrical Length of a Wire

Electrical length is the wire length expressed in wavelengths:

Electrical Length = Wire Length / λ

  • 0.25 λ → quarter-wave
  • 0.5 λ → half-wave (EFHW)
  • 0.625 λ → 5/8-wave (low-angle DX)
  • 1.0+ λ → long-wire behavior
Key Insight: Electrical length determines impedance, radiation angle, and transformer requirements.

2. Feedpoint Impedance Modeling

Vertical antennas exhibit different impedances depending on electrical length:

Electrical LengthTypical ImpedanceNotes
< 0.2 λ600–1500 ΩVery short, inefficient
0.25 λ30–70 ΩQuarter-wave vertical
0.5 λ2000–5000 ΩEFHW
0.6–0.7 λ100–300 Ω5/8-wave DX geometry
1.0+ λ300–1200 ΩRandom-wire behavior

2.1 Why Rybakov Lengths Work

They avoid resonance points, keeping impedance moderate and tunable.

3. Transformer / UNUN Theory

Transformers convert high impedance to something closer to 50 Ω.

3.1 Impedance Transformation

Z_out = Z_in / (ratio²)

  • 49:1 → divides impedance by 2401
  • 64:1 → divides impedance by 4096
  • 9:1 → divides impedance by 81
  • 4:1 → divides impedance by 16
Example:
A 2500 Ω EFHW feedpoint becomes ~52 Ω through a 49:1 transformer.

4. Coax Transformation

Coax acts as an impedance transformer when its electrical length is significant.

4.1 Electrical Length of Coax

Electrical Length = (Physical Length × VF) / λ

4.2 Transformation Formula

Z_in = Z0 × (Z_load + jZ0 tanβl) / (Z0 + jZ_load tanβl)

The HF Vertical Designer uses a simplified model for speed, but the behavior is directionally accurate.

5. SWR Calculation

SWR is derived from the reflection coefficient:

Γ = (Z - 50) / (Z + 50)
SWR = (1 + |Γ|) / (1 - |Γ|)

5.1 With Tuner

A tuner reduces SWR at the radio, but not at the feedpoint. The tool assumes a modern ATU can reduce SWR to ~1.5:1.

6. ERP Modeling

ERP is computed from:

  • Transmitter power
  • Antenna gain (height + type)
  • Ground conductivity
  • Coax loss
  • SWR mismatch loss

6.1 Mismatch Loss

Mismatch Loss (dB) = -10 log10(1 - |Γ|²)

6.2 Height Gain

Higher antennas produce lower takeoff angles → more DX gain.

6.3 Saltwater Gain

Saltwater ground plane improves low-angle radiation by ~3 dB.

Example:
A 25 ft Rybakov at 100 W near saltwater can produce ERP equivalent to 250–300 W inland.

7.1 Azimuth Pattern

7.2 Elevation Pattern
